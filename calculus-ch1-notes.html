Chapter 1: Applications of Functions of One Variable

Limits, Continuity, Differentiability, Mean Value Theorems, Integration and Applications

1. Limits

The concept of a limit describes the behavior of a function as the independent variable approaches a particular value.

Definition

If the value of f(x) approaches L as x approaches a, then:

lim (x → a) f(x) = L

Types of Limits

  • Left-hand limit (LHL): lim (x → a⁻) f(x)
  • Right-hand limit (RHL): lim (x → a⁺) f(x)
  • Two-sided limit: Exists if LHL = RHL
Example: lim (x → 2) (x²) = 4

2. Continuity

A function is said to be continuous at a point if it has no break or jump at that point.

Conditions for Continuity at x = a

  • f(a) is defined
  • lim (x → a) f(x) exists
  • lim (x → a) f(x) = f(a)
Polynomials, exponential and trigonometric functions are continuous everywhere in their domains.

3. Differentiability

Differentiability measures the rate of change of a function and represents the slope of the tangent to the curve.

Definition

f'(x) = lim (h → 0) [f(x + h) − f(x)] / h

Important Points

  • If a function is differentiable at a point, it is continuous at that point.
  • The converse is not always true.

4. Mean Value Theorems

Mean Value Theorems connect the average rate of change of a function to its instantaneous rate of change.

Rolle’s Theorem

  • f(a) = f(b)
  • f(x) is continuous on [a, b]
  • f(x) is differentiable on (a, b)

Then, ∃ c ∈ (a, b) such that f'(c) = 0

Lagrange’s Mean Value Theorem

f'(c) = [f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a)

5. Riemann Integration

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation and is used to find areas under curves.

Definite Integral

∫ab f(x) dx

It represents the signed area between the curve y = f(x) and the x-axis from x = a to x = b.

6. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This theorem establishes a connection between differentiation and integration.

Part I

If F'(x) = f(x), then:

∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)

7. Applications of Integration

Area

Area bounded by y = f(x) and x-axis:

Area = ∫ f(x) dx

Length of Curve

L = ∫ab √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx

Volume of Revolution

V = π ∫ y² dx

Surface Area of Revolution

S = 2π ∫ y √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx
