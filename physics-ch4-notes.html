1. Introduction to Low Dimensional Materials

Low dimensional materials are materials in which the motion of charge carriers (electrons and holes) is confined in one or more spatial dimensions to the nanometer scale. When at least one dimension of a material becomes comparable to the de Broglie wavelength of electrons, classical physics fails and quantum mechanical effects dominate.

These materials exhibit unique electrical, optical, thermal, and mechanical properties that are not observed in bulk materials.

3. Quantum Confinement Effect

Quantum confinement occurs when the dimensions of a material are reduced to the order of the electron‚Äôs de Broglie wavelength. As a result, the motion of electrons becomes restricted, leading to discrete energy levels.

  • Stronger confinement ‚Üí larger energy gap
  • Energy levels depend on size of structure
  • Explains color tuning in quantum dots

4. Classification of Low Dimensional Materials

(a) Quantum Well (2D)

In quantum wells, carriers are confined in one dimension and free to move in the other two. This results in discrete energy levels in one direction.

  • Used in semiconductor lasers
  • High electron mobility

(b) Quantum Wire (1D)

In quantum wires, carriers are confined in two dimensions and free to move in only one dimension. This leads to one-dimensional density of states.

  • Used in nanoscale transistors
  • Improved transport properties

(c) Quantum Dot (0D)

Quantum dots confine charge carriers in all three dimensions. They behave like artificial atoms with discrete energy levels.

  • Size-dependent optical properties
  • Sharp emission spectra
üìå Differences between Quantum Well, Wire and Dot are frequently asked.

5. Synthesis Techniques

  • Bottom-Up: Chemical vapor deposition (CVD), molecular beam epitaxy (MBE)

6. Properties of Low Dimensional Materials

  • Optical: Tunable band gap
  • Thermal: Reduced thermal conductivity
  • Mechanical: High strength-to-weight ratio
