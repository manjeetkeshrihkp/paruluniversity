1. Need for Band Theory

In isolated atoms, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. However, in solids, millions of atoms are closely packed and their outer orbitals overlap. As a result, discrete energy levels split into continuous energy bands.

Classical theories like Drude‚ÄìLorentz theory failed to explain:

  • Temperature dependence of conductivity
  • Difference between conductors, semiconductors, and insulators
  • Quantum behavior of electrons

üëâ Band Theory, based on quantum mechanics, successfully explains these phenomena.

4. E‚Äìk Diagram

The E‚Äìk diagram represents the variation of electron energy (E) with wave vector (k). It gives information about:

  • Electron velocity
  • Effective mass
  • Nature of band gap

Curvature of E‚Äìk diagram decides the effective mass of charge carriers.

5. Direct & Indirect Band Gap Semiconductors

Direct Band Gap

CB minimum and VB maximum occur at the same k value. Electron transition releases energy directly as photon.

  • High light emission efficiency
  • Used in LEDs & Lasers
  • Examples: GaAs, InP

Indirect Band Gap

CB minimum and VB maximum occur at different k values. Phonon involvement causes energy loss as heat.

  • Poor light emission
  • Used in electronic devices
  • Examples: Si, Ge

6. Effective Mass of Electron

In a crystal lattice, electrons experience periodic potential. Their motion differs from free electrons and is described using effective mass (m*).

Effective mass depends on curvature of E‚Äìk diagram:

Higher curvature ‚Üí Lower effective mass ‚Üí Higher mobility

Important: Effective mass explains why electrons and holes move differently inside semiconductors.

7. Fermi Energy & Fermi Level

Fermi energy is the highest occupied energy level at absolute zero (0 K). Fermi level indicates probability of electron occupancy.

Material Fermi Level Position
Conductor Inside conduction band
Semiconductor Near middle of band gap
Insulator Deep inside band gap

8. Intrinsic & Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor

  • Pure semiconductor
  • Equal electrons and holes
  • Low conductivity

Extrinsic Semiconductor

  • n-type: Pentavalent doping, electrons majority
  • p-type: Trivalent doping, holes majority
